Apo Island

The Island

Small island. Big ocean.

Apo Island sits about 7 km off the southern tip of Negros Oriental in the central Philippines. It's a tiny volcanic cone — barely 12 hectares — ringed by some of the healthiest coral reefs in Southeast Asia. The community here declared a marine sanctuary back in 1982, long before it became fashionable, and the results speak for themselves: the water is full of life.

There are no roads, no cars, and no resorts eating up the coastline. What you get instead is a single fishing village, a handful of homestays, and an underwater world that draws divers and snorkellers from all over the globe. Sea turtles are so common here they barely notice you.

The Rusti Shack

Where the crossing ends and the day begins.

The shack started simple — a few snorkels, a mask or two, and a surfboard propped against the wall for whoever felt brave enough. Word got around that you could grab your gear right at the water's edge without lugging it over from the mainland, and the little pile of rentals grew from there.

Today we stock everything from beginner snorkel sets to fishing rods, rashguards to reef-safe sunscreen — but the spirit is the same. Come off the bangka, grab what you need, and get in the water. We'll look after the rest.

Getting Here

How to reach Apo Island by bangka.

There's only one way in — by bangka, the traditional outrigger boat of the Philippines. The crossing takes 30–45 minutes depending on conditions, and it's a beautiful ride through open water with the island slowly coming into view. Here's how to piece the journey together.

1

Fly into Dumaguete (DGT)

Dumaguete is the nearest city with an airport. Cebu Pacific and Philippine Airlines both fly in from Manila and Cebu. The city is relaxed and worth a night if you're arriving late.

2

Head south to Malatapay or Dauin

Take a tricycle or hire a van south from Dumaguete — about 30–40 minutes. Most bangkas to Apo Island depart from Malatapay wharf. Wednesday is market day at Malatapay, which makes for a great stop along the way.

💡 Agree on the tricycle fare before you get in — around ₱200–300 from Dumaguete.
3

Board the bangka

Regular bangkas run from Malatapay to Apo Island most mornings, with the last departure typically around midday. Charter boats are also available if you want to set your own schedule. The crossing takes 30–45 minutes.

💡 Seas can get choppy in the afternoon — aim to cross in the morning if you can.
4

Land at the island — find the shack

The bangka pulls up to the beach at the main village. Walk up from the water and you'll find us right there — The Rusti Shack, the little gear shop at the edge of the sand. Pick up what you need and you're ready to go.
